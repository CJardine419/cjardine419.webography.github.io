Waste

Source 1

Website URL https://www.forbes.com/sites/aishanyandoro/2025/10/20/the-rush-to-build-ai-data-centers-leaves-black-workers-with-new-risks/
Author Aisha Nyandoro
Abstract An article from Forbes examines the environmental and social consequences of waste generated by artificial intelligence infrastructure, particularly data centers. As AI systems evolve, hardware must be frequently replaced, leading to a growing volume of electronic waste. This waste often contains hazardous materials that can contribute to pollution and health risks if not properly managed. The article also emphasizes that these impacts are not evenly distributed, as communities located near data centers—often low-income—experience higher exposure to environmental hazards. This perspective highlights the intersection of environmental sustainability and social justice, demonstrating how technological growth can reinforce existing inequalities if not carefully regulated.

Source 2

Website URL https://hbr.org/2024/07/the-uneven-distribution-of-ais-environmental-impacts
Authors Shaolei Ren and Adam Wierman
Abstract Research from Harvard Business Review focuses on the carbon footprint and lifecycle impact of AI systems, emphasizing how waste extends beyond physical materials to include energy-related emissions. Training and maintaining AI models require significant computational power, which contributes to greenhouse gas emissions and environmental degradation. The article highlights that as AI adoption increases, the cumulative environmental cost of these systems continues to grow. This perspective expands the concept of waste by including both physical e-waste and invisible environmental impacts, such as emissions. It underscores the importance of considering the full lifecycle of AI technologies when evaluating their sustainability.
